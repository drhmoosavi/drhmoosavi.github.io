Research scope

In metastatic cancers, tumors spread and evolve in ways that create a patchwork of different cell populations. This "molecular heterogeneity" makes some cells survive treatments that kill others. Metastasis itself unfolds as a series of steps—escape from the primary site, survival in the blood, seeding new tissues—each step shaped by evolutionary pressures that select for the fittest clones. Finally, patient-derived organoids (PDOs) are mini-tumors grown from a patient's own cells; by testing drugs on these organoids, it is possible to predict which therapies will work best and pinpoint biomarkers that flag response or resistance.

Research projects

Molecular tumor heterogeneity in metastatic cancers

Metastatic lesions often harbor multiple genetic subclones that coexist and compete, meaning that even within one patient, different regions of the same cancer can behave very differently under treatment. This diversity arises from ongoing mutations and epigenetic shifts as cells invade new tissues, leading to pockets of drug-resistant cells that fuel relapse.

Cancer metastasis development and molecular evolution

Cancer cells leave the primary tumor, survive in circulation, and colonize distant organs through a cascade of steps—each stage applying selective pressure that shapes the genetic makeup of emerging metastases. Recent genetic analyses show that in many cancers, metastatic spread starts years before diagnosis, with metastases often arising from early subclones rather than the dominant primary clone.

Modeling treatment response in patient-derived tumor organoids and biomarker discovery

PDOs are three-dimensional cultures made from a patient's own tumor tissue that faithfully capture its genetic and cellular complexity. By screening drugs on these organoids, researchers can predict which treatments a patient's tumor will respond to and identify biomarkers—molecular flags—that forecast sensitivity or resistance
